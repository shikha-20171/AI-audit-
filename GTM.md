# GTM

The exact target user is a founder, CTO, or engineering manager at a 10–80 person startup that already spends real money on AI tooling but does not yet have a procurement function. The best version of this user is a technical founder at Seed to Series A, or an engineering manager at a venture-backed company where engineers can expense tools quickly and finance only notices once the monthly SaaS bill becomes messy. They are not looking for “AI strategy.” They are trying to answer a narrower question: are we paying too much for the stack we already have?

Right before they want this tool, they are usually doing one of a few things: checking a surprise OpenAI or Anthropic invoice, debating whether to buy more Cursor or Copilot seats, cleaning up software spend before a board update, or comparing tools in public threads like “Cursor vs Copilot vs Claude Code.” The search intent is not “AI spend audit.” It is “ChatGPT Team pricing,” “Cursor business worth it,” “Claude Team minimum seats,” “OpenAI API cheaper than ChatGPT Team,” or “best AI coding tool for startup team.”

Where they hang out online is specific. On Reddit: `r/startups`, `r/SaaS`, `r/SideProject`, `r/Entrepreneur`, and sometimes `r/LocalLLaMA` when API cost questions come up. On X: founder circles, indie hackers, and engineering leads who post their tooling stack screenshots. In communities: Indie Hackers, small private founder Slacks, startup CTO WhatsApp groups, and operator communities where finance and engineering overlap. Product Hunt and Hacker News matter for launch-day reach, but they are not where the first ten users come from. The first ten come from warm and semi-warm circles where someone is already complaining about AI bills.

With $0 paid budget, my first 100 users in 30 days would come from five moves. First, post a brutally concrete launch thread on X with example audits, not a vague “we built a tool” teaser. Second, turn three anonymized audits into mini teardown posts and share them on Indie Hackers and relevant founder Slacks. Third, DM founders who publicly mention AI tooling costs and offer a free manual audit plus the self-serve link. Fourth, publish one Hacker News post framed around “we built a free AI tooling benchmark/audit for startup teams” and stay active in comments. Fifth, ask Credex customers or prospects to run their current stack through it and share the public result with peers.

The unfair channel is Credex itself. Credex already sits close to the buyers who have the biggest pain: teams spending enough on AI infrastructure that discounted credits matter. That means the company can seed the tool with distribution through its own network, outbound conversations, and social proof from actual savings cases. A random startup could build this calculator, but only Credex can directly close the loop from “you are overspending” to “here is a concrete way to buy cheaper capacity.”

If this works in week 1, traction should look like: 300 landing visitors, 90 completed audits, 25 email captures, 8 high-savings audits above $500/month, and 3 consultation calls booked. Those are not vanity numbers. They would prove people understand the value proposition, trust the output enough to share contact details, and generate a lead pool with real savings potential.
